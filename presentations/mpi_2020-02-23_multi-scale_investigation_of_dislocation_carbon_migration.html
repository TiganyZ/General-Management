Introduction

  • Rolling contact on bearing raceways causes degradation in subsurface microstructure.
  • Failure by Rolling Contact Fatigue (RCF).
  • Degradation arises in form of Dark Etching Regions (DERs).
  • DERs characterised by development of ferrite and carbide features with patches of unaltered martensitic matrix.

Circumferential section showing DER [Fu2017].


Features of DER

  1. Martensite → ferrite microbands (from strain localisation).
  2. Residual carbides gradually dissolve.
  3. 30° and 80° ferrite features form: White Etching Bands (WEBs).
  4. Lenticular carbides precipitate at ferrite band boundaries.
  5. Carbides thicken during DER development; correlated with WEB growth.
  6. Reduction in dislocation density in late stage.

Kernel Average Misorientation (KAM) map showing 30° & 80° WEBs and carbide features [Smelova2017].


Unresolved problems

  • Where does excess carbon from the martensitic matrix find itself when the structure decays to low C solubility (0.02 wt%) ferrite?

  • How is carbon transported, given its low diffusivity in martensite/DER phases?


  • Carbon redistribution and plastic deformation are thought to be fundamental mechanisms behind DER formation.
  • Differing mechanisms of dislocation-driven carbon migration have been suggested, but no consensus.
  • Can use multi-scale modelling to ascertain the mechanism of dislocation-assisted carbon migration.

Dislocation-assisted carbon migration

Cyclic stresses caused by bearing use causes dislocations to drag carbon [Fu2017].

  • Carbon segregated to dislocations.
  • Cyclic stresses cause dislocations to break away from carbon environment.

  • Carbon re-attracted to dislocation, causing carbon flux.


  • Modelling can find regimes of stress/temperature/carbon concentration/dislocation density in which this is feasible.

Methods

  • Quantum-mechanical, atomistic (tight-binding/TB) simulations used to see how solutes interact with dislocations.
    • Tight-binding is more accurate than empirical potentials, with better scaling than DFT.
  • Line tension model of dislocations used to ascertain how carbon interactions change dislocation movement.
  • Future work: SCkMC model to simulate dislocation dynamics and measure effects on dislocation velocity and loop debris in carbon environment. [Katzarov2017]


Atomistic results

Core reconstruction

  • C transforms the easy screw core, the ground-state core structure in pure Fe, to hard core.
  • The hard screw core is the ground-state dislocation configuration when carbon is dissolved in ferrite.
  • Empirical potentials only predict core spreading, no spontaneous reconstruction to hard core.

  • C-dislocation binding energies and C-C repulsive energies can be used to find the concentration of carbon on dislocation line at given T.

Diagram: C reconstruction of easy screw core § to hard [Ventelon2015]. Video: Tight-binding simulation of reconstruction.


Binding of C to screw core

Binding sites of carbon around the easy core dislocation in TB.

Binding sites of carbon around the hard core dislocation in TB.

  • Highest binding energy found inside hard core.
  • Cores fixed in position, such that reconstruction does not occur.
  • Binding to both cores important for line-tension model: to determine C concentration at which hard core becomes dominant.

Concentration analysis

Carbon concentration on dislocation line

  • Equilibrium carbon concentration on dislocation line from the C-dislocation binding energies.
  • Can include the effect of the C-C first-neighbour repulsive energy of carbon within hard-core prismatic site, which gives a more realistic estimation using the bare McClean Isotherm for the equlilbrium concentration.

What does this tell us?

  • Dislocation cores are saturated with carbon around normal operating temperature (320\(\deg\) K), and dislocation densities even up to maximum measured in martensite \(\sim 5\times10^{15}\).
  • This means all dislocations are reconstructed to the hard core configuration in our material of interest.
  • How do different concentrations of carbon affect dislocation movement?

Line-tension Results

Line-tension model of dislocation

\[ E_{\text{LT}} = \frac{K}{2} \sum_j (\vec{P}_j - \vec{P}_{j+1} )^2 + \sum_j \Delta E_{\text{P}} (\vec{P}_j) + (\sigma \cdot \vec{b}) \times \vec{l} \cdot \vec{P}_j - \sum_{j,k} E_{\text{C}} (|\vec{P}_j-\vec{P}_k^{\text{C}}|), \]

  • Equation used to obtain the line energy for the line tension model.
  • We can obtain the kink-pair formation enthalpy as a function of stress, temperature and carbon concentration.
  • The interaction between solutes is parameterised with a lorentzian.

  • String method used to find transition state image.

Figure 3: Kink-pair formation in Peierls landscape. [Itakura2012]


Kink-pair formation enthalpies

  • The addition of carbon reduces the kink-pair formation enthalpy.
  • The reduction is less than one would expect from hydrodgen as the interaction with carbon is longer ranged due to the large tetragonal distortion and binding energies.

Future Work

SCkMC Simulations

  • Kinetic Monte Carlo simulations incorporating carbon diffusion will complete the description of how carbon can move with dislocations

  • Figure of kMC work with hydrogen (blue), showing dislocation line (black) with generated debris loops (red) during movement of dislocation line [Katzarov2017b].

Summary

  • Used tight-binding model of Fe-C, rooted in quantum mechanics, to simulate dislocation-C interactions.
  • Hard core found to be ground-state in system with carbon.
  • Equilibrium concentration of carbon on dislocation line predicts that all dislocations are hard core, at typical dislocation densities, nominal carbon concentrations and operating temperatures.
  • Line-tension model shows that carbon decreases kink-pair formation enthalpy barrier: it is easier for dislocations to move in a carbon environment.
  • Carbon decreases the stress necessary to move the screw dislocations at all temperatures.
