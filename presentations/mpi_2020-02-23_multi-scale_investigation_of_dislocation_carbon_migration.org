* Introduction
** Introduction
   - Rolling contact on bearing raceways causes /degradation in subsurface/ microstructure.
   - Failure by Rolling Contact Fatigue (RCF).
   - Degradation arises in form of /Dark Etching Regions/ (DERs).
   - DERs characterised by development of ferrite and carbide features with
     patches of unaltered martensitic matrix.

** Features of DER
   #+REVEAL_HTML: <div class="column" style="float:left; width: 48%">
   1) Martensite → ferrite microbands (from strain localisation).
   2) Residual carbides gradually dissolve.
   3) 30\deg and 80\deg ferrite features form: White Etching Bands (WEBs).
   4) Lenticular carbides precipitate at ferrite band boundaries.
   5) These carbides thicken during DER development; correlated with WEB
      growth.

** Unanswered questions
   1) Where does excess carbon from the martensitic matrix find itself
      when the structure decays to low C solubility (0.02 wt%) ferrite?
      #+REVEAL_HTML: <br/>
   2) How is carbon transported, given its low diffusivity in
      martensite/DER phases?
      #+REVEAL_HTML: <br/>
   3) Do pre-existing carbides dissolve or thicken?
      #+REVEAL_HTML: <br/>
   */Dislocation-assisted carbon migration/* has been suggested as
      fundamental mechanism, but no proof/consensus.

* Dislocation-assisted carbon migration
** Dislocation-assisted carbon migration
   #+REVEAL_HTML: <div class="column" style="float:left; width: 48%">
[[file:~/Documents/docs/Management/fe_skf_paper/sebastian/Images/dislocation-assisted_carbon_migration_mechanism_fu2017_transparent.png]]
 Cyclic stresses caused by bearing use causes dislocations to drag carbon cite:Fu2017.
   #+REVEAL_HTML: </div>
   #+REVEAL_HTML: <div class="column" style="float:right; width: 48%">
   - Carbon segregated to dislocations.
   - Cyclic stresses cause dislocations to break away from carbon environment.
   - Carbon re-attracted to dislocation, causing carbon flux.
     #+REVEAL_HTML: <br />
   - Modelling can find regimes of stress/temperature/carbon concentration/dislocation density in
     which this is feasible.
   #+REVEAL_HTML: </div>

** Why is it plausible?
   - Explains:
     - Martensite to ferrite transformation.
     - Carbon transport.
     - Lenticular carbide growth.
     - Pre-existing carbide dissolution.
   - Can use _multi-scale modelling_ to ascertain if mechanism of
     dislocation-assisted carbon migration is valid.

* Multi-Scale Modelling
** Methods
    - _Quantum-mechanical_, atomistic (tight-binding/TB) simulations used
      to see how solutes interact with dislocations.
      - Tight-binding is more accurate than empirical potentials, with
	better scaling than DFT.
    - _Line tension model_ of dislocations used to ascertain how carbon interactions modify
      thermally-activated dislocation movement (kink-pair formation).
    - _SCkMC model_ to simulate dislocation dynamics and measure
      effects on dislocation velocity and loop debris in carbon
      environment. cite:Katzarov2017

  # ** DER Mechanisms
   
  #    - Theories of carbon redistribution causing DERs come from several
  #      observations:
  #      - Ferrite formation
  #      - Lenticular carbide formation
  #      - Dissolution of residual carbides.
  #      - Difference in C concentration from original martensite to DER.
  #    - Dislocations can attract carbon and form Cottrell atmospheres.
  #    - Current theories suggest with dislocation glide, carbon
  #      migration is assisted.
  #    # - Growth of lenticular carbides is linked to formation of DER through
  #    #   mechanical diffusion by dislocations and thermal diffusion.
  #    - Debate as to whether carbon is deposited in temper carbides,
  #      causing them to grow, or if there is dissolution of temper
  #      carbides.


  # ** New DER Mechanism proposal

  #    - Dislocation pile-ups could cause attract carbon from temper/residual
  #      carbides causing their dissolution.
  #    - Dislocation rearrangement into cell structures from cyclic
  #      stresses and dislocation-carbon interaction.
  #    - Large plastic strains in region will form ferrite microbands and
  #      nanocrystalline ferrite with as the number of stress cycles
  #      increases.
  #    - Expelled C from ferrite microbands and nanocrystalline ferrite
  #      forms lenticular carbides.
  #    - Carbon diffusion assisted by pipe diffusion along dislocation.

     #   - Does C actually move with the dislocation?

* Line-tension Results
** Line-tension model of dislocation

   \begin{equation}
   E_{\text{LT}} = \underbrace{
   \underbrace{\frac{K}{2} \sum_j (\vec{P}_j -
   \vec{P}_{j+1} )^2}_{\text{Spring term}}
   + \underbrace{\sum_j \Delta E_{\text{P}}  (\vec{P}_j)}_{\text{Peierls Potential}}  -
     \underbrace{\sum_{j,k} E_{\text{C}}
     (|\vec{P}_j-\vec{P}_k^{\text{C}}|)}_{\text{C Interaction}}}_{\textbf{From Atomistics}}
+ (\sigma
     \cdot \vec{b}) \times \vec{l} \cdot \vec{P}_j,
v
     \end{equation}
       #+REVEAL_HTML: <div class="column" style="float:left; width: 60%">
      - We can obtain the kink-pair formation enthalpy as a function of
	stress, temperature and carbon concentration.
      - All terms from atomistic data, apart from Peach-Kohler term.
      - The interaction between solutes is parameterised with a lorentzian.

* Summary
  - Dark Etching Regions are formed during Rolling Cycle Fatigue of
    bearing steels, but no consensus on mechanism.
  - Multi-scale modelling can shed light on the validity of
    dislocation-assisted carbon migration.
  - Atomistics finds the _hard screw core_ to be _ground-state_
    due to carbon interaction.
  - _*All* carbon segregated to dislocations_, during martensite
    → ferrite transition, due to large binding energy of C.
  - Therefore, initial assumption of */dislocation-driven carbon migration/* is correct.
  - _All dislocations_, even in _high-purity iron_, are _pinned with
    carbon in a hard core configuration_ (up to 400\deg K).
  - Line-tension model shows amount by which _carbon decreases kink-pair formation
    enthalpy_ barrier if ahead of dislocation. Drag if behind.
  - Further work, through SCkMC model will ascertain regimes in which
    dislocation-assisted carbon migration is valid.
